English Text

Cities should invest more in neighborhood clothing-repair workshops and craft classes. Many people think these services are old-fashioned, but I believe they are valuable for modern urban life.

First and foremost, repair skills reduce waste. Fast fashion encourages people to throw away a jacket when a button falls off or a sleeve tears. Yet a tailor can often fix such problems in one afternoon. A well-made coat or waistcoat may even last for years if someone repairs it at the right time. In environmental terms, keeping one piece of clothing in use is often superior to buying two cheap replacements.

Second, repair work helps local people earn income in practical ways. A retired tailor, a fashion student, or a small shop owner can offer simple services such as shortening trousers, replacing linings, or adjusting formal clothes for weddings and job interviews. These tasks may seem minor, but together they create steady business. If cities provide low-rent workshop space or short training programs, more residents will be able to earn income without needing a large company behind them. It also teaches young people that useful manual work deserves respect, not the lower status it often receives in office-centered societies.

Finally, repair culture strengthens community ties. When people get in touch with a local craftsperson, they learn to value skill and patience. One customer may arrive because a grandfather's waistcoat no longer fits; another may bring a school uniform that has been used by brothers for years. In both cases, the repair is not only technical. It is also personal. Once people see this, they are bound to treat clothes more carefully and buy fewer things they do not need.

For these reasons, support for repair workshops should not be seen as nostalgia. It is a practical policy for cleaner cities, stronger local business, and more thoughtful consumption.
